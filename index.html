Local Agency Accused of
Making Brands Actually
Worth Talking About

Witnesses report "unnerving levels of strategic clarity" and "campaigns that do not insult the customer." Authorities are investigating.
By THE EDITORIAL BOARD · Filed at 4:17 AM · Filed late, as usual

In a development that has reportedly stunned the marketing establishment, a scrappy agency operating out of a converted print shop in Brooklyn has been linked to a string of brand launches that "people under forty could describe without rolling their eyes." The agency, known to associates only as The Broadside Tribune, is said to approach every engagement as if it were a newspaper assignment: gather the facts, find the story, and — critically — make the reader care before asking them to buy anything.

Former clients describe the experience as "disorienting, because they asked us questions before showing us a deck." One founder, speaking on condition of anonymity, admitted the agency refused to deliver a "brand video with soft piano" and instead wrote a campaign that "made our customers email us unprompted." Sales are reportedly up. Nobody is more surprised than the CMO.

The Tribune's creative director, reached for comment, declined to "monetize vibes" but did offer the following: "If your customer cannot tell a friend what you do in one sentence, we have not done our job, and also you should not have hired a skywriter." Sources close to the matter confirm this is a direct quote, delivered while setting type by hand.

About the Paper

— A2 —

We are a marketing agency pretending to be a newspaper, because we believe the best brands earn their ink. Everything we do — strategy, identity, launch, campaign, copy — is designed to be reported on rather than merely announced. If a press release cannot survive the editing process of a decent metro desk, we throw it out and write it again. That is the job.

Our founders met at a college paper in 2011, spent a decade each in the kind of agencies that bill for "synergy moments," and quietly started taking freelance briefs on the side. In 2019, the moonlighting became the business. In 2021, we stopped doing slideshows and bought a printing press. In 2024, we turned down our biggest retainer because the brief used the word "ideate" fourteen times. We remain in business, somehow.

We work on five things at a time. Never six. We write everything out loud before we write it on a screen. We assign each client to a real human with a real phone number who answers it. We have never, and will never, invoice for a "discovery workshop."

If the above sounds like a pitch, consider this your subscription card. We look forward to your assignment desk.

EXCLUSIVE REPORT · BEVERAGE

How a Kombucha Company Stopped Talking About Kombucha

Florin & Finch was dying in Whole Foods. Six months later, shelf velocity tripled. The product never changed. Here is what did.
By D. OKONKWO · Managing Editor · Filed March 2026
PHOTO: A bottle, a label, and an opinion about fermentation.

When Florin & Finch came to us in Q4, their deck contained seventeen slides about their ferment. It contained zero slides about the person drinking it. The category had commoditized. Every SKU on the shelf shouted "probiotics," "gut health," and "raw." Nobody was shouting anything human.

We reframed the brand around a single, undefended observation: people who drink kombucha at 3pm are not trying to heal their gut. They are trying to survive Wednesday. We rewrote the entire system — label, voice, flavor names — around that insight.

We stopped selling a beverage and started selling a 3pm in a bottle.

The rebrand launched in eleven weeks. Shelf-facing copy dropped every word related to wellness. The flavor "Ginger Lemon" was renamed "The Meeting After The Meeting." Early indicators were cautiously positive. Late indicators were loud.

INVESTIGATION · FINTECH

A B2B Fintech Admits, Finally, What It Actually Does

After eleven months of calling itself a "platform," Meridian Ledger agreed to say "we help CFOs close the books faster." Traffic doubled inside a week.
By M. HOLLOWAY · Publisher · Filed January 2026
PHOTO: One version took 340 words to explain the product. The other took 9.

Meridian Ledger had $34M in ARR and a homepage that nobody in the sales funnel could summarize. The hero headline was "Infrastructure for Modern Finance." The subhead was longer. The screenshot was a dashboard of a dashboard.

We did not rebrand. We did not redesign. We wrote a single sentence, got the CEO to approve it in one call, and rewrote the top of every page and every ad against it. That sentence was: "Meridian closes your books three days faster, without changing your ERP."

Clarity is not a visual problem. It is a willingness problem.

Within a week, qualified demo requests from the homepage increased 94%. The sales team asked us to also rewrite their cold emails. We did. Conversion on outbound replies rose in parallel. Nobody has asked us to "punch up the copy" since.

FEATURE · D2C APPAREL

The Sock Company That Stopped Running Black Friday

Against all agency advice — including some of ours — Hastings & Co. canceled their November promotion. Margin recovered. So did the brand.
By J. SATO · Art Director · Filed December 2025
PHOTO: The campaign that wasn't. Print-only. One run.

Hastings had trained its customer to wait until November. Margin was decaying 4pts a year. We argued for a hard break: no discount, no promo, one print-only campaign mailed to 40,000 existing customers explaining why.

It worked. Not immediately. Q4 revenue was flat year over year. But Q1 — the quiet quarter — came in 41% above plan. The newsletter open rate, previously 18%, held at 46% for eleven consecutive weeks.

The campaign that does not run is sometimes the one that wins the year.

Discounting, we have come to believe, is a tax a brand pays on the sentences it was too tired to write.

PROFILE · HOSPITALITY

How a 14-Room Hotel in Maine Outbooked Its Competitors

The Aldworth had no budget, no brand, and a website built in 2014. We gave it a voice. Bookings followed.
By R. WHITFIELD · Staff Writer · Filed September 2025
PHOTO: No models. No drone shots. Just the hotel and the light in October.

The Aldworth's previous copy described "coastal luxury." So did every other page on the first screen of Google. We rewrote every room description as a short story. We photographed the fog. We removed the words "curated" and "crafted" from the entire site.

Direct bookings, previously 22% of inventory, rose to 61% over the following year. The property raised rates twice. Guests started mentioning specific sentences from the website in their reviews.

A hotel is not sold by the thread count. It is sold by the story on the pillow.

We now keep an open line to the front desk. They call when a sentence stops working. We rewrite it. The cycle takes a day.

Marketing Ideas
That Passed This Year
JAN 2025 — APR 2026 · R.I.P.

It is with solemn regret that we report the deaths, quiet and overdue, of the following. The industry observes a moment of silence, interrupted only by someone's Slack notification.

  • The Gradient That Won't Quit. Survived by every SaaS homepage. Beloved by nobody.
  • "Authentic," the Adjective. Worked hard for twelve years. Meant nothing by 2019.
  • Mascot-as-Strategy. Briefly resurrected by AI. Put back down.
  • The 4-Minute Brand Video with Piano. Autoplayed to empty rooms. Nobody paused.
  • "We're Not Just a Company, We're a Community." A community of one slack channel.
  • The All-Lowercase Rebrand of 2021. Rebranded again, in Title Case, this year.
  • Stock Photo Handshakes. Still working. Send help.
  • The QR Code on the Super Bowl Ad. Scanned eight times. Four by the agency.
  • "Ideate." It was never alive. We never should have said it.
